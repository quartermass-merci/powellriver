Scratchpad
Recording. Speak naturally.
Rules of engagement
  • Never read questions off a sheet. Know the core five. Let them come out naturally. Chase prompts are there if someone stalls or gives a surface answer.
  • Stories over opinions. If someone says "it's great," chase: "Tell me about a specific time." Every adjective should be backed by a scene.
  • Record everything. Audio always. Video where appropriate. MOTS get phone video at minimum.
  • Capture the sentence, not the paragraph. Flag anything you'd put on a billboard with a ★ so we can find it in transcript review.
  • Don't correct or educate. If someone misunderstands what Meridian does, that's data. The awareness gap is a research finding.
  • Chase the true-ish. If someone says there were cage fights at Forrest, or UFO sightings over Powell Lake, or that the building is haunted — that's gold. Don't fact-check in the moment. Capture it, note the source, move on.
  • The landscape is an actor. Powell Lake, the Hulks, the ferry, the rain, the isolation. These aren't backdrop — they're characters. When someone mentions them, follow it.
  • Consent before cameras. Verbal for audio, written release for any footage we may use externally. Carry release forms.
Tier 1 · Universal core

The spine of every conversation. MOTS use Q1–Q3 and close with Q5. Formals use all five, with room to breathe.

  1. Q1. "What comes to mind when you think of Powell River?"
    Chase: "What's one thing about this place that surprised you — or that surprises people who visit?"
    Opens place narrative. Works for residents, employees, outsiders.
  2. Q2. "What do you know about what's happening at the old mill site?"
    Chase: "How did you first hear about it?" or "What have people around you said about it?"
    Tests the awareness gap. Delta between reality and perception is our most important finding.
  3. Q3. "If you had to describe Meridian to someone who'd never heard of it, what would you say?"
    Chase (outsiders): "What would you guess a cannabis company inside a former paper mill is like?"
    Brand perception baseline. The guess itself is diagnostic.
  4. Q4. "What's changed here in the last five years?"
    Chase: "Is that change something people are proud of, worried about, or both?"
    Surfaces tension between heritage and transformation. Feeds the "second life" narrative.
  5. Q5. "What would you want someone from outside this community to understand about this place?"
    Chase: "What do people get wrong about Powell River?"
    Emotional closer. Surfaces pride, protectiveness, and NIMBYism signals.
MOTS quick card

For Mike + Katie street intercepts. Three questions, two minutes, one usable clip.

Open: "We're doing a story about Powell River. What comes to mind when you think of this place?"
Probe: "Do you know what's happening at the old mill site? What have you heard?"
Story: "What's the weirdest or most unexplainable thing about living here?"
Close: "What should someone from outside this community understand about it?"
If they mention cannabis or Meridian unprompted: "How would you describe them to a friend?"
If the story question lands: "Where did you hear that? Do other people know about it?"

Story layer, always on

Not a tier. A posture. Cards in your back pocket, dropped in when the moment opens up. Target: 20+ true-ish stories by Day 3.

QS1. "What's the weirdest thing about this place?" Gateway question. Low stakes, high yield.
QS2. "Does this place have ghosts? Not just in the mill — anywhere." The eerie. Ghosts in the Machine for employees; the past-that-won't-leave for locals.
QS3. "If these walls could talk, what would they say?" Historical mythology. Sante Veritas, Tilt, the American company that walked away.
QS4. "What do people come to Powell River to escape?" The draft dodger question, broadened. Back-to-the-landers, deliberate isolation, the grow culture that predates legalization.
QS5. "What does this place do to people who stay?" Place-as-forge. Isolation breeds self-reliance. The type it produces is the brand's emotional territory.
QS6. "What's the thing about Powell River that no one writes about but everyone here knows?" The deep cut. Use late, after trust is built. Ideally not on camera.
Collection protocol. Log every story in the Scratchpad above or a meeting's Field Notes. Each entry: the story, who told it, where, when, conviction rating 1–3. Cross-reference: if two people independently tell the same one, it's a myth that matters.

Field Guide · Confidential

The Weird & The Eerie

A field guide to the weird corpus of Powell River — UFOs, Sasquatch, hermits, burned homesteads, a roadside cult, and restless ghosts. From Toba Inlet down through Powell Lake, the town, the Malaspina Channel, and across to Texada. What does this place believe about itself?

Walk the decades
The pattern markers
  • Silence at altitude. Every close-range UFO account notes no engine sound.
  • NW flight vector. Where direction is specified, it's northwest.
  • Hair-covered humanoids in European folklore. Basajaun, marfolat, skograa, hulder. The Ostman encounter has deep antecedents.
  • Hancock House is the house publisher. Green, Mobley, Belyk — a local bibliographic tradition, not an accident.
The 1970s as hinge
  • Olsen Valley burned, 1972.
  • First Sunshine Coast UFO cluster, 1975–78.
  • Ostman dies, 1975.
  • Anderson's sawmill sold, 1976.
  • Courthouse Inn converted from government use, 1974.
  • Ostman's case enters John Green's canon, 1978.
